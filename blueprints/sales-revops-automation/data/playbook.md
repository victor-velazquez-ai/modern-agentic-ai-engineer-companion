# Winning-messaging playbook

A small corpus of past-winning follow-up snippets and account-history notes. The
`draft_outreach` workflow retrieves from this with the `rag-pipeline` blueprint so drafts are
*grounded in what has actually worked*, not invented. In your own deployment, replace this with
your won-deal email corpus, call summaries, and account history.

## Security review follow-up (worked: Globex, Initech-style deals)

When a security or compliance review is the gating next step, the highest-converting follow-up
names the exact artifacts and attaches them: the SOC2 Type II report and a one-page data-
residency overview. Keep it short, route it to the security contact, and restate the close date
so the timeline stays anchored. Deals where we attached the SOC2 packet within 24 hours of the
call closed 30% faster.

## Build-vs-buy decision follow-up (worked: enterprise eng buyers)

When a technical buyer has decided to buy rather than build, the winning follow-up sends a
tailored technical evaluation plan, not a generic deck. Reference the buyer's stated launch
deadline, propose concrete evaluation milestones, and offer to loop in their security lead
early so the review does not become a last-minute blocker.

## Pricing and budget sign-off follow-up

When a champion has a number in mind but needs finance sign-off, give them a one-page business
case they can forward: the year-one cost, the expected payback, and two reference customers in
their industry. Make it forwardable. Champions who got a forwardable business case advanced to
proposal 2x more often.

## Data-residency / regulated-industry messaging

For financial-services and EU buyers, lead with in-region data storage and SOC2. Never promise
a residency guarantee you have not confirmed with the platform team; state what is supported and
offer a written statement. Over-promising on residency is the single most common deal-killer in
regulated accounts.

## Tone and structure

- Open by referencing one specific thing the buyer said on the call (shows you listened).
- State the single agreed next step and who owns it.
- Restate the timeline / close date if one was discussed.
- Keep it under 150 words; a busy buyer forwards a short email, not a long one.
- Sign as the rep, not as an automated system.
