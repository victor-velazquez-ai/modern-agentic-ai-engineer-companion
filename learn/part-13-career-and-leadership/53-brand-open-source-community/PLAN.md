# Ch 53 — Personal Brand, Open Source & Community

> Companion plan · Part XIII · book file `chapters/53-brand-open-source-community.typ`
> Status: 📋 planned (Phase 1)

## Role in the companion
This chapter argues for the engineer's version of a public record — "a public, verifiable
record that you can do the work" — built from writing, open source, and networking treated as
*learning exhaust*, not a second job. The companion is **one worksheet**: a concrete
"building-in-public" plan plus the chapter's three checklists turned into a recurring,
self-auditing cadence. There is **no notebook by design** — the work is publishing and
contributing on a schedule, which a worksheet plans and a notebook can't perform.

> **Why a worksheet, not a notebook (per REPO-PLAN §4 + the pedagogy guardrail):** reputation is
> built by *consistently shipping artifacts*, not by executing code. The highest-value asset
> here is a sustainable plan (topic niche, monthly cadence, open-source tier, networking moves)
> with a checklist to hold the line against perfectionism and performative volume. This chapter
> is flagged reference/worksheet-only in REPO-PLAN §4; we honor that rather than invent code.

## Planned worksheets

### 53-01 · `53-01-building-in-public-plan.ipynb` — Your publishing + OSS + networking cadence
- **Type:** worksheet  *(markdown prompts + fill-in cells + checklists; no executable code)*
- **Maps to:** book §53.1 (writing, speaking, building in public), §53.2 (open-source
  strategy & reputation), §53.3 (networking that compounds) — closes on the §53 checklist.
- **Objective:** commit to a *sustainable* public-record plan — a niche, a monthly "learning
  exhaust" artifact, a chosen open-source tier, and a giving-first networking habit — and
  install the checklist that keeps it consistent over two years rather than two weeks.
- **Prereqs:** book Ch 53 read; the capstone / real work from earlier parts as raw material to
  publish about. No tooling.
- **Cell arc:**
  - 🧠 the engineer's brand = *a public, verifiable record you can do the work* (not influencer
    theater); state your one consistent niche and the platforms you'll be findable on.
  - "Learning exhaust" plan: list four things you debugged, built, measured, or decided
    recently — each is a post you can write *for yourself one month ago* (the perishable
    beginner-memory advantage). Pick the next one and its genre (debugging story / honest eval
    with numbers / explanation at altitude).
  - 🔮 *predict* which failure mode is yours — **perfectionism** (the six-weeks-polished,
    never-shipped post) or **performative volume** (daily recycled platitudes) — then write the
    one rule that counters it ("one substantive post a month for two years beats both").
  - Open-source tier choice: pick *one* tier honestly — focused contributions to one tool you
    use → owning a niche tool → significant maintainership — with its cost/return and your
    first three concrete actions (per-community reputation compounds where you stay).
  - 🎯 senior lens: curate the repo as a *demonstration of the judgment layer* — flagship README
    explains the *why*, ADRs + eval suite present, abandoned experiments archived (as code gen
    gets cheap, this is what a repo uniquely proves).
  - Networking-that-compounds plan: a give-first list (a community-Slack answer, a stranger's
    code review, an intro), the *one* community to engage deeply, and a light-maintenance
    cadence ("saw this, thought of your project" twice a year).
  - The highest-return move: when you publish, send it to the three people whose work it builds
    on, with one specific sentence on what you took — draft that message now.
  - 📋 self-audit against the §53 checklist (one substantive artifact last month / quarter?
    a project where maintainers know your name? flagship repo shows judgment not just code?
    findable + consistent niche? three people helped with no return + three who'd vouch?).
- **Datasets/fixtures:** none — the reader's own work and network are the subject; output is a
  cadence plan + a recurring checklist the reader keeps.
- **APIs & cost:** none/offline — planning and reflection only; no model calls, no posting, no
  secrets (any actual publishing happens outside the repo, by the reader).
- **You'll be able to:** run a sustainable building-in-public cadence — a niche, a monthly
  artifact, one OSS tier, and giving-first networking — with a checklist that defeats both
  perfectionism and performative volume.
